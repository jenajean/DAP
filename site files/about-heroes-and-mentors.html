Heroes and Mentors

I want to honor the people in the world of pianos that have inspired, challenged, intimidated, and taught me.

Jack Coffer

In late 1974, in Atlanta, Georgia, I was trying to figure out what I was going to do other than play 250 rock and roll dates a year on the road, living a less-than-optimum lifestyle, if you take my meaning. My wife had a child on the way, and I wasn't going to be an absentee parent; that wasn't an option for me, even though I dearly loved music and had been a successful full-time player for many years.
I had always had this thing in the back of my mind that I could tune pianos if I ever needed something else to do; I always loved the piano, laying underneath our family grand (a Mehlin & Sons 6'2", a good piano, the same quality for the time it was manufactured---1926---as a Yamaha C series piano would be today) as my mother would play---everything from Bach to Errol Garner transcriptions to show tunes. Lying underneath, I was enveloped, immersed, in the massive, echo-ey sound.
I called some piano stores and one rebuilding shop out of the Atlanta phone book: nothing. No openings, nobody looking for an apprentice, no interest. Zip. The next day, I called one more rebuilding shop, and the guy I talked to referred me to a piano tuning class that was being offered through a little city college deep in the heart of downtown Atlanta. I signed up.
In a dingy classroom on a rainy evening, Jack Coffer strode in the room. He was tall, a bit stooped, wiry and sharp-faced. He went to an old upright piano with the front off, sat down, and started in, explaining and showing the basics of aural piano tuning. I was transfixed, and stayed after class. Jack and I hit it off, big time.
After several weeks, amazingly, I had embodied the basics of tuning, and could tune a piano reasonably well---and I loved it; I loved solving the puzzle of the temperament, pulling in unisons, listening in an almost hyperfocused way, loved the whole thing. Jack was smart, funny, no-nonsense. He stressed relaxing. He told me I had talent, that my body knew how to do this; I just needed to pay attention and get better. I trusted him and I got a lot better really fast.
Within a month I was working full-time at the dealer Jack did work for (Cecil White & Sons Pianos) and I stayed there, working with him, soaking everything up, for 2 years. He was my first teacher, and gave me a lot of habits and approaches, most good, some not so good. I will always be grateful to him for recognizing and supporting my gift for pianos; he was a hard-charging, rough-edged, kind-hearted man.

Virgil Smith

Certainly one of the best-known piano technicians in the United States, he was a piano technician and music teacher in the Chicago area for 6 decades. He was the foremost proponent of what he called "natural, whole-tone aural tuning." I use a tuning method which is founded on and supported by his method; you could say it's very, very similar to his system. Virgil championed what he called complete piano service for many years, and I am an acolyte of his in the sense that I follow, almost to the letter based on my 30 years' experience, the protocols he employs (and has written about in various articles and pamphlets over the years, in the clearest, simplest, most elegant language) to keep a piano in the best condition with minimal service after the initial comprehensive setup.
This man was truly a hero of mine: smart, funny, kind-hearted, rational, a great teacher, one of the best tuners in the world, and he worked well into his nineties. Really precise, concert-level piano tuning is a lifelong learning process, and Virgil allowed me to perceive the beauty of my own work and inspired me to get better and better, to never be satisfied with my work. I'm forever grateful to him for this. Thank you, Virgil.

Richard Davenport

A titan in our profession, I've had the privilege of being close to his work for more than two decades. Playing a Steinway "B" he rebuilt in 1986 literally changed my life. I had been preparing and setting up new Steinways for the local Los Angeles dealer, Sherman Clay, since 1983. In early 1987, I walked into a store called Merrill's Music, in downtown Santa Monica, looking for a piano for a client of mine. The "B" Richard had rebuilt was there, on consignment. When I played it, I was dumbfounded----it sounded and felt so much better than the dozens and dozens of new B's I'd worked on in the four years prior to this day. Wow. I couldn't believe it. Not only was the tone open, soaring, powerful, the action felt fantastic---light, even, responsive, sensual; so, so balanced.
That epiphany forced me into a deeper understanding of the action of a piano, and into the realization that I had a long, long way to go as a technician. Richard has been the standard by which many of us who aspire to the high end have judged and measured ourselves. He has taught and shared his rare blend of gifts with other piano technicians for decades, in classes at state, regional and national PTG conferences; his standards and ethics are impeccable. He is one of the very, very few in the world who are capable of the highest levels of concert and recording work AND the highest levels of rebuilding work---a true "Renaissance man" of pianos.
Davenport is currently in private practice in Los Angeles, and is affiliated with Renner, maker of action parts, and Fazioli, maker of the world's most expensive pianos.

Norman Neblett

One of Richard Davenport's main mentors, "tuner to the stars," he maintained many of the crucial performance pianos in L.A. through the '60's, '70's, and 80's. One of the founders of what is now the Piano Technician's Guild, Norman has been honored with the highest award the PTG can bestow: the Golden Hammer Award. He was warm, cranky, generous, amazingly attractive to women of all ages, and a fountain of piano knowledge. It has been a particular privelege of mine that I got to know him personally before he died, after having sat in brilliant classes and seminars of his over the years.

Randy Morton and Mick Farnell

These guys are the principals of Pacific Piano Supply, a local supply house here in southern California, and they have been my friends and mentors and occasional collaborators for years. I've learned a lot from both of them---they are unselfish and generous with their time and expertise, and they're simply honest, steady, trustworthy, good-hearted men who give our business a good name. I'm proud to be their colleague.

Bill Garlick

One of the founders of perhaps the best school of piano technology in North America, the North Bennett Street School, a recognized expert regarding 18th and 19th century instruments, and director of technical services for Steinway & Sons from 1981 until 1986. I met him at a 2-week-long seminar at the Steinway factory in 1984, and it was a revelation. I learned as much or more in the 2 weeks I spent with him than in the previous 10 years combined. He is an excellent teacher and storyteller, and he captured my attention in a profound way.

Michael Spreeman

This incredibly complex, brilliant, rough, demanding, beautifully open-hearted and honest man heads a team of artisans in making, in my opinion, the finest one-off, from-scratch, custom-made pianos in the world. Full stop.
If you haven’t seen or heard a Ravenscroft 7-or-9-foot piano, it’s hard to fully comprehend the power and clarity, and the physical beauty, of these instruments. They’re made one at a time, with the utmost attention to every detail in every realm. Stevie Wonder asked Michael, after playing a 7-foot Model 225, jokingly, “Did you make this piano just for me?”
Spreeman is generous with his time and energy, always teaching other piano technologists at the local, regional, national, and global levels, either formally or privately. He’s a successful and loving husband and father, with a wide circle of people all over the world who love and support him. He is a force for good in this world, for art, for beauty, for consciousness. I am proud to call him my friend, confidant, teacher and mentor.
And: he’s funny as hell, and brings out the funny in me: a great and good friend.

David Stanwood

If there is one person that has revolutionized the way piano designers, engineers, tuners, technicians, and players have dealt with and perceived Spielart---a beautiful word that only exists in German, meaning “the entire feeling/perception of both tone and touch when the piano is played”---it is David Stanwood.
His Equation of Balance was first revealed and started having profound effects on action rebuilding 20 years ago. Since then, the Stanwood protocols for achieving ideal touchweight in a piano action have been discussed, debated, fought over, universally accepted, and now are part of the DNA of piano building AND rebuilding around the world.
The premium resource in the world for piano parts and supplies is Jahn Pianoteile, in Germany; they have officially declared Stanwood’s Precision Touch Design their preferred method of piano action rebuilding and performance enhancement. They sell the tools made specifically for the Stanwood method, and they are champions of his work.
Stanwood’s brilliant new invention, the Stanwood Adjustable Leverage Action, or SALA, is the first true retrofit game-changer in the piano business in decades. It allows the pianist to customize the touch of a piano instantly, with a few turns of two knobs; the left hand and the right hand are independently adjustable, and there is no change of regulation specifications at all, so the secure feeling of the player stays throughout an astonishing range of “lightness to heaviness” or “less resistance to more resistance.”
One concert pianist and legendary teacher called it “ a completely new and different way of expressing emotion when I play different things. It’s brilliant. Profoundly brilliant.” (Click on the blue SALA above to find out more…)
I have the honor & privilege to be partners with Mr. Stanwood in a company called SALApianos, Inc. We have located distributor/installers around the globe, and are actively involved in selling the SALA device.
David is a brilliant, warm, quirky, deeply ethical and honest man, and a fine business partner. We have fun and do what we love….

David Abell

I thought I had a special relationship with David Abell, who many people have called the template for what a successful piano seller should be.  When he died, in February of 2006, I suddenly discovered he had the same kind of intimate, fatherly, “working” relationship, as an honored mentor, with dozens, maybe hundreds of people. He has been my conscience in this business for 25 years; so much of what I have achieved has been as a result of wanting to do the right thing in David Abell’s eyes.  He was a hero in the truest sense of the word.
As a practical example of the Golden Rule personified, I’ve never met better.  His death created a tremendous void in the piano world.    "[I want to thank] someone who is not here this evening, but is responsible for showing me this path. He showed a young music major the amazing transformation a piano goes through in the hands of a “professional piano technician”. Time and time again, he showed me the joys a musician experienced playing a beautifully prepared instrument. He showed me the value of educating the novice about the workings of a piano and why they deserved better than grandma's upright spinet grand that "never needed tuning." He taught me selling quality and service was sometimes harder but it was the right thing to do.
He ignited a passion by example. Although not a technician himself, he supported PTG and hired some of our best & brightest to work on his pianos. His pianos were always prepared for the pianist---beginner and professional alike. His reputation for service among musicians is legendary. If the piano came from him there was no need for concern. He had the highest of standards and was abundant in praise if the instrument had merit and constructively critical if it didn't. He was especially tough on the manufacturers he loved and cared so much about; he tried to get them to produce the best pianos possible.
Over the years, he has helped raise millions of dollars to support young musicians by way of scholarships and instrument loans, often with the help of his friends at Yamaha and Bosendorfer. He felt giving back to the community was of the utmost importance. David Abell was an advocate for the technicians, the pianists and the piano makers, but, in the end, it was for his love of music that he worked so tirelessly. In the end, everything we do is for the music.
Kayoko Forrest, RPT in a speech at the California State PTG Conference, February 11, 2006, the night David Abell died
